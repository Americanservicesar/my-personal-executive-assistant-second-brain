## 1. S-Corporation Election (Potential Savings: $10,000–$25,000/year)

**What it is:** ASAR could elect S-Corp status with the IRS (Form 2553). This converts the single-member LLC to an S-Corp for tax purposes.

**How it saves money:**
- Currently, all ASAR net profit flows through to Anthony's personal return and is subject to self-employment (SE) tax of ~15.3% on the first ~$160K and 2.9% above that.
- With S-Corp election: Anthony puts himself on payroll at a "reasonable salary" (e.g., $50,000/year). Only that salary is subject to SE/payroll taxes.
- Remaining net profit flows through as a distribution — NOT subject to SE tax.
- If ASAR earns $150,000 net profit: SE tax on $150K ≈ $21,240. With S-Corp at $50K salary: SE tax on $50K ≈ $7,650. **Savings ≈ $13,590/year.**

**What it requires:**
- File Form 2553 with IRS (deadlines matter — typically by March 15 for current year, or within 75 days of forming)
- Set up payroll (Gusto, QuickBooks Payroll, etc.)
- Anthony must receive a "reasonable compensation" salary
- Annual S-Corp tax return (Form 1120-S) + Anthony's K-1
- AR state S-Corp income tax return

**When it makes sense:** Generally worth the added complexity when net profit exceeds ~$40,000/year.

**Questions for CPA:**
- Is ASAR profitable enough to benefit now?
- What is the IRS-defensible "reasonable salary" for Anthony's role?
- Can we still do a late S-Corp election for 2026?
- Does this affect the sales tax delinquency situation at all?

## 2. Arkansas Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET) (Potential Savings: $2,000–$8,000/year)

**What it is:** Arkansas enacted a PTET election (Act 362 of 2023) allowing S-Corps and partnerships to pay state income tax at the entity level.

**How it saves money:**
- The SALT (State and Local Tax) deduction on Schedule A is capped at $10,000 for individual taxpayers since 2017.
- If ASAR (as S-Corp) pays AR state income tax directly, that payment is a 100% deductible business expense on the federal return — not subject to the $10K SALT cap.
- AR individual income tax rate: 4.4% (2024 rate after recent reductions)
- On $100,000 of ASAR income: $4,400 AR state tax. If paid at entity level = $4,400 federal deduction at ~22% bracket = ~$968 federal tax saved.
- On higher income the savings compound further.

**What it requires:**
- Must have S-Corp election in place first (see Idea #1)
- Make the PTET election on the AR state S-Corp return
- Pay estimated PTET payments quarterly

**Questions for CPA:**
- What is the PTET election deadline for 2026?
- At what income level does PTET become meaningfully valuable for Anthony?

## 3. Section 179 + 100% Bonus Depreciation (Equipment Write-Offs)

**What it is:** Under the One Big Beautiful Budget Act (OBBBA, enacted 2025), 100% bonus depreciation is **permanent** for qualifying property placed in service after January 19, 2025.

**How it saves money:**
- ASAR buys a pressure washer trailer rig for $18,000.
- Normally: depreciated over 5-7 years = ~$2,571/year deduction.
- With 100% bonus depreciation: **Entire $18,000 deducted in year of purchase.**
- At 22% tax bracket + 15.3% SE tax = effective rate ~37% → $18,000 deduction saves ~$6,660 in taxes in year 1.

**What qualifies:**
- Pressure washers, trailers, surface cleaners, hot water units
- Trucks and vans (may have luxury auto limits — check with CPA)
- Most tangible business property with useful life ≤ 20 years
- Does NOT apply to land or most buildings

**Planning opportunity:**
- If ASAR needs equipment: buy it before year-end to get the deduction in the current tax year
- If ASAR has a high-income year: accelerate equipment purchases into that year
- If ASAR has a low-income year: may want to spread deductions (use Section 179 with carryforward if needed)

**Questions for CPA:**
- What equipment purchases are planned for 2026? Coordinate timing with tax strategy.
- Is there a vehicle purchase in the plan? (Luxury auto limits apply to passenger vehicles.)
- Should ASAR use 100% bonus depreciation or Section 179 for each purchase?

## 4. Kids on Payroll (Potential Savings: $3,000–$8,000/year)

**What it is:** If Anthony has children (ages ~7-17), they can be employed by ASAR for legitimate work and paid a reasonable wage.

**How it saves money:**
- Child's wages are deductible as a business expense (reduces Anthony's taxable income).
- Child pays tax at their own rate — if under the standard deduction threshold (~$14,600 in 2025), they owe **$0 federal income tax.**
- If ASAR is a sole proprietorship or single-member LLC (not S-Corp): children under 18 are also exempt from FICA (Social Security/Medicare) taxes.
- Example: Pay each child $12,000/year for legitimate work. That's $12,000 deduction for Anthony at ~37% effective rate = ~$4,440 tax savings per child. Child pays $0 tax.

**Legitimate work examples for ASAR:**
- Social media content (photos, video for jobs)
- Stuffing envelopes, preparing mailers
- Entering job data into spreadsheets
- Cleaning/organizing the shop or equipment storage
- Filing and admin work
- YouTube/social media assistant (if old enough)

**Requirements:**
- Work must be real and age-appropriate
- Wages must be reasonable for the work performed
- Keep records: time worked, tasks, pay stubs
- Open a custodial checking account for the child
- If S-Corp: FICA exemption may not apply — consult CPA

**Questions for CPA:**
- Does Anthony have children at appropriate ages?
- What is the FICA exemption status if/when S-Corp election is made?
- What documentation is needed to substantiate the employment?

## 5. Augusta Rule (Home Office for Meetings)

**What it is:** Under IRC Section 280A(g), a homeowner can rent their home to their business for up to 14 days per year tax-free — meaning the homeowner (Anthony) pays no income tax on the rental income, and the business (ASAR) deducts the rental expense.

**How it saves money:**
- ASAR pays Anthony's home a "rental fee" for using it as a business meeting location (team meetings, client strategy sessions, planning meetings, etc.).
- Fair market rental rate for comparable meeting space in Conway/Little Rock: estimate $500–$1,500/day for a quality space.
- 14 days × $1,000/day = $14,000 deductible business expense for ASAR.
- Anthony receives $14,000 tax-free (no personal income tax on home rental ≤ 14 days).
- At 37% effective tax rate: ~$5,180 tax savings.

**Requirements:**
- Must have actual business meetings at the home (documented)
- Rate must be based on comparable commercial rental rates (get a quote from a local event venue)
- Keep records: meeting dates, attendees, agendas, invoices from ASAR to Anthony
- Maximum 14 days per year (the 15th day makes ALL rental income taxable)
- Must be a real business meeting — not personal gatherings

**Questions for CPA:**
- Is Anthony's home suitable for documenting as a meeting venue?
- What is the defensible fair market rate for meeting space in his area?
- Does this strategy hold up to IRS scrutiny at ASAR's income level?

## 6. Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction — Ongoing

**What it is:** Section 199A allows pass-through business owners (sole props, S-Corps, partnerships) to deduct up to 20% of qualified business income from federal taxable income.

**Current status:** This deduction was set to expire after 2025 but was made **permanent** under the OBBBA (2025).

**How it helps ASAR:**
- If ASAR has $100,000 QBI: 20% deduction = $20,000 off taxable income
- At 22% bracket: saves ~$4,400 in federal income tax
- Service businesses (like ASAR) may hit income phase-out limits — consult CPA

**Questions for CPA:**
- Does ASAR's business type qualify fully for QBI (some service businesses are limited)?
- At what income level does the phase-out begin for Anthony's filing status?
- Is the QBI deduction being taken on Anthony's current returns?

## 7. Retirement Accounts (SEP-IRA or Solo 401k)

**What it is:** As a self-employed business owner, Anthony can contribute to a SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k) — both reduce taxable income significantly.

**Contribution limits (2025):**
- SEP-IRA: Up to 25% of net self-employment income, max $69,000
- Solo 401(k): Up to $23,500 employee contribution + 25% employer contribution, max $69,000 total (higher if over 50)

**How it saves money:**
- Contributions are pre-tax: reduce Anthony's taxable income dollar-for-dollar
- Example: $20,000 SEP-IRA contribution at 37% effective rate = ~$7,400 tax savings

**Note:** Anthony already has a 401(k) (from prior employment or wife's plan) and Edward Jones account. Confirm with CPA how these interact with a business retirement account.

**Questions for CPA:**
- Does Anthony currently have a SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k) for ASAR?
- What is the maximum contribution for 2025 based on ASAR's net income?
- How does this interact with the existing 401(k) and Edward Jones accounts?
