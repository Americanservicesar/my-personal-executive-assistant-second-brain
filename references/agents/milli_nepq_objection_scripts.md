# Milli — NEPQ Objection & Rejection Response Scripts
**Source:** NEPQ Black Book of Questions by Jeremy Miner (7th Level Inc., 2024)
**Purpose:** Milli's rejection handling framework — never counter, always question back

---

## Core NEPQ Philosophy

- **Never argue or defend.** Arguing inflames resistance. Asking questions diffuses it.
- **Internal tension > External pressure.** When YOU tell them why to buy, they resist. When THEY tell you, they believe it.
- **85% Engagement / 10% Presentation / 5% Commitment.** Most of the conversation is questions, not pitching.
- **Tonality:** Curious, slightly confused — like a doctor asking about symptoms. Not pushy. Not defensive.
- **Key rule:** When they come to their own conclusions, they not only accept them — they fight for them.

---

## The 6 Core Objection Scripts (ASAR-Adapted)

---

### 1. "Just send me a quote / price"

**Never:** Send a number cold — it anchors them before they see value.

**Milli says:**
> "Yeah for sure — just so I can make sure the quote actually makes sense for your property, can I ask what you're looking to have done and roughly the size of the area? That way I'm not sending you a number that's way off."

→ Now they're talking. You're in the engagement stage. Find their problem before presenting the solution.

**If they push back ("I just want a ballpark"):**
> "Totally get it. Can I ask — what's prompting you to look at this right now? Just so I can understand what you're working with."

---

### 2. "I need to think about it"

**Never:** "No problem, let me know!" → you never hear from them again.

**Milli says:**
> "Totally makes sense. Can I ask — what specifically is it that you'd want to think through? Just so I understand where you're at."

→ Forces them to name the real objection. Price? Timing? Not convinced? Now you know.

**Follow-up if they say "just thinking":**
> "Is it more about the timing, or is it something about what I shared that didn't quite land right?"

---

### 3. "Too expensive / I can get it cheaper"

**Never:** "We're competitively priced and offer great value..." → sounds like every other vendor.

**Milli says:**
> "I hear you. Can I ask — if the price wasn't a factor, is this something you'd want to move forward with?"

→ If yes: budget is the only issue → solve it.
→ If no: deeper objection — uncover it.

**Follow-up:**
> "What would it take for this to make sense for you?"

**If they truly have no budget:**
> "I can appreciate that money might be an issue. What other avenues do you have to find the funds so that you can [repeat back what they said they wanted]?"

---

### 4. "I'll call you back / I'm busy right now"

**Never:** "Okay, no problem!" → they don't call back.

**Milli says:**
> "Not a problem at all — rather than playing phone tag, would it be easier if I sent you a link to grab a specific time? That way you don't have to chase me down either."

→ Calendar commitment. Keeps them in the funnel with a specific time locked.

**If they say "I'll get back to you sometime this week":**
> "With my schedule it might be hard to randomly connect. If you have your calendar handy I can pull up mine — that way we both have a time locked in. Would that work?"

---

### 5. "Send info to my email, I'll look at it"

**Never:** Send info → silence → chase forever.

**Milli says:**
> "Yeah, I can do that for sure — just so I send the right stuff, what specifically were you hoping to see? Like ballpark pricing, before/afters, what we cover?"

→ They answer → you're in a conversation again.

**After brief engagement, circle back:**
> "Good — so let me put together something specific to what you mentioned. Let's say you get it and it looks like it fits what you're looking for — what would you want the next step to be?"

---

### 6. "We already have someone who does that"

**Never:** Bash the competitor or oversell ASAR.

**Milli says:**
> "Oh nice — how long have you been with them?"

*(Let them answer)*
> "What do you like about working with them?"

*(Let them answer)*
> "What would you change if you could?"

→ The last question is the key. They'll tell you their frustration. That's your opening.

## Bonus: Vendor Disruption Questions

Use when they're happy with their current vendor but you want to plant a seed:

1. *"So to me it sounds like things are 100% perfect with what you're using now — what would you change if you could?"* (emphasize "100% perfect" — they almost always backpedal)
2. *"In what ways could your current vendor do better for you than what they're doing now?"*
3. *"Back when you chose them, what were your selection criteria? In what ways has that changed as you look at your situation today?"*

## Complaint / Upset Customer (Cassie also uses this)

**Never argue. Never defend. Ask until they feel heard.**

1. *"Can you tell me exactly what happened so I understand?"*
2. *"What else can you tell me about this?"*
3. *"I apologize that this happened. What would you like to see done at this point so we can make this right?"*
4. *"Would it help if I did some fact-finding and then we got back together to go over some options?"*

→ Goal: win the relationship, not the argument.

---

## Qualifying Questions (Before Investing Time)

Use these before committing to a full proposal or site visit:

> *"Let's say we were able to solve the [problem they mentioned] — what do you see happening next?"*

> *"How important is it for you to do something about this? Why now, though?"*

> *"Are you prepared to change your situation so you can [repeat back what they said they wanted]?"*

If their answer to "what happens next" is vague ("we'd have to run it by someone", "not sure") — don't invest heavy time yet. Qualify further first.

---

## Proposal Rules (NEPQ)

1. **Never send a proposal without understanding their problems AND confirming they have budget.** If they ask for one upfront: *"I'd be open to putting one together — but just so you know, I'm not sure we can even help you yet. It might make more sense if I asked a few quick questions first, so the proposal actually fits your situation. Would that help?"*

2. **Proposals should reference the 2-3 problems THEY told you.** Not features. Not ASAR's awards. Their stated pain.

3. **Always offer 3 options:** Basic (lower price, still profitable) / Core (most common) / Premium (full package). Let them choose — most land in the middle.
